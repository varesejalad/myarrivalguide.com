Then we had to find somewhere to sleep.

Chapter 1

Before you fly to Australia

The stuff to sort out while you're still at home. Get this right and your first week in Australia is way smoother.

Australia has two working holiday visas and they're not the same. Which one you qualify for depends entirely on your passport, not your age or background.

The Subclass 417 visa is for most European passports — UK, Germany, France, Italy, Netherlands, Estonia, Ireland, Belgium, and more. Easier requirements. No farm work needed for first year extension in some cases.

The Subclass 462 visa is for countries like USA, Spain, Portugal, Argentina, Brazil, and several Asian and South American nations. Slightly different requirements.

Both let you stay 12 months and extend up to 3 years. The experience inside Australia is identical. The only real difference is the application paperwork and which countries can apply.

My experience

I spent honestly a week confused about this when I first started looking into it. It seems complicated but it's not — go to the Australian Home Affairs website, click the visa finder, and it tells you exactly which one applies to you in 30 seconds. Don't read random Reddit threads that might be outdated.

You apply directly through the Australian Government's ImmiAccount portal. It's a normal government form — there is no shortcut, no special service worth paying for, and no insider trick.

You'll need: a valid passport with at least 12 months left, proof of around AUD $5,000 in savings (a bank statement is fine), and basic personal details. The application fee is currently AUD $635.

Processing time varies but most people get approved within a few days to two weeks. Some get approved in hours.

  • Apply at least 3 months before you want to fly
  • Your passport must be valid for the full year of your stay
  • Don't book flights until your visa is granted
My experience

I genuinely cannot stress this enough — do NOT pay anyone to "process your visa" for you. There are companies that charge $200-500 to fill in the same form you can fill in yourself in 30 minutes. They're not faster. They're not better. They're not approved by the Australian government. It's just a markup. Save the money for your first month in Australia.

The Australian government technically requires you to show $5,000 AUD in savings to be granted the visa, but what you actually need to survive your first month is a different conversation entirely.

Australia is expensive. Especially accommodation. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise. Real numbers from Perth, based on actual experience:

  • 7 nights Airbnb in the suburbs (not even CBD): ~$1,000 AUD
  • Hotel when Airbnb runs out and you're desperate: expensive and you have no choice
  • Food and basics for first 2 weeks: $300-400
  • SIM card, transport, admin: $100-150
  • Car from Facebook Marketplace (essential in Perth): $3,000-6,000

The car situation alone changes everything. In Perth especially, you need a car. Public transport exists but it's not enough for getting to farm work or moving around properly. Budget for a car from week 2-3 onwards.

Bottom line: come with at least $5,000 AUD if you can. $3,000 is survivable but stressful. Less than that and you're in a tough spot before you've even started looking for work.

I came with around €3,000 which wasn't enough. The first week was fine — Airbnb in Joondalup, getting SIM and bank sorted. But when that Airbnb ended and we had nowhere to go, we ended up paying over the odds at a hotel because there were no cheap options left. Then we slept in the car for a while. Your home bank card will work in Australia but the exchange rates and foreign transaction fees will quietly eat your savings. A Wise or Revolut card uses the real exchange rate and charges almost nothing for currency conversion.

Wise (formerly TransferWise) gives you a multi-currency account that holds AUD, EUR, GBP, USD, and dozens more. You load it once from your home bank, then spend in Australia at the actual mid-market rate.

Revolut is similar with slightly different features. Both work fine. Wise is generally considered slightly better for travelers because of cleaner exchange rates and no monthly limits.

  • Set it up at home — verification is much easier with a home address
  • Load it with €500-1000 equivalent before you fly
  • Use it for any spending in your first week before you open an Aussie bank account
My experience

Wise was genuinely one of the best decisions I made before flying. Used it from day one in Australia — every ATM, every tap payment, worked perfectly. The exchange rate was noticeably better than my home bank every single time. Set it up before you leave because verifying your identity is easier from home. Honestly one of the things I'd tell every person coming here: get Wise before you fly, load it up, and stop worrying about exchange rates.

This is the step most backpackers skip because they think they'll be fine. Most of them are. But a small percentage discover too late that Australian medical bills without insurance are genuinely catastrophic.

The critical thing: not all travel insurance covers you while working. A standard tourist policy will not pay out if you get hurt at a farm job or in hospitality. You need a policy that specifically mentions working holiday visa holders.

The big providers for WHV travelers are: True Traveller, SafetyWing, World Nomads, and Cover-More. Prices range roughly $50-150 per month depending on age and coverage level.

  • Make sure it covers manual labor / farm work specifically
  • Check the medical evacuation limit — should be at least $250k AUD
  • Read what counts as a "pre-existing condition"
My experience

I didn't get insurance for my first 3 months and I got lucky. Please don't be me. One backpacker in my hostel broke his leg falling off a hostel bunk bed — his bill was over $8,000 AUD just for the ambulance, x-rays, and a basic cast. That was without any complications. Don't gamble on this.

Where you land first shapes your whole first month. Each major city has a different personality and a different reality for backpackers.

Sydney

Biggest job market, biggest backpacker scene, most expensive. Hospitality work is everywhere. Bondi and Coogee for beach life, inner west for culture, CBD for jobs. Rent in shared houses is $250-400/week.

Melbourne

Cheaper than Sydney, way more culture and food scene, weather is famously unpredictable. Trams everywhere. Cafés are world class. Rent is $200-350/week.

Brisbane

Warm year round, more relaxed, growing fast, easy to get around. Less competition for jobs. Closer to farm work. Rent is $200-300/week.

Perth

Well paid (especially construction and mining), beautiful beaches, but very isolated from rest of Australia. Flights from Perth to anywhere else cost more.

My experience

I started in Perth because I had connections there. It was beautiful but isolated — getting to anywhere else in Australia costs more from Perth than from any other major city. Each city has different advantages. Pick based on whether you prioritize jobs, weather, cost, or social scene.

Showing up in Australia without somewhere to sleep is a recipe for stress. Even more importantly — you need a local address before you can open a bank account, get a SIM contract, or apply for jobs. Your hostel address is that first local address.

Book through Hostelworld or Booking.com for the first 3-5 nights. Hostelworld is more focused on backpacker hostels specifically. Booking has more variety including budget hotels.

What to look for in your first hostel:

  • Central location (easy to walk to job interviews, banks, ATO offices)
  • Free wifi (essential for everything you need to do)
  • Kitchen access (food costs add up fast)
  • Good reviews from other backpackers, not just tourists
My experience

I booked an Airbnb in Joondalup for the first week — split with my mate it cost around $330 each. Honestly I'd recommend booking at least 2 weeks before you arrive. We had to scramble after the first week ended and ended up paying way more than we should have.

Your home country driver's licence is valid in Australia as a visitor, but the rules on how long are inconsistent between states. This catches people out.

  • NSW — Your foreign licence is valid as long as you're a "temporary overseas visitor". WHV holders technically qualify but it's a grey area at 3-6 months.
  • Victoria — 6 months from arrival on a permanent visa, but WHV holders can use it for the duration of the visa.
  • Queensland — Valid for as long as you're on a WHV.
  • Western Australia — 3 months as a visitor, then you need to convert.

If your licence is not in English, you need an International Driving Permit (IDP) which you should get in your home country before flying. Some backpackers also use NAATI translation services to translate their licence into English officially.

My experience

We had to get our licences translated through NAATI when we arrived in Perth — found someone offering this service through Facebook. Check your specific state on the Service NSW / VicRoads / etc website before driving long-term.

Australia has an enormous backpacker community and most of it organizes through Facebook. Joining the right groups before you arrive means you land already plugged into the network.

The most useful ones tend to be:

  • State-specific WHV groups — "Working Holiday Australia Sydney", "Backpackers in Melbourne", etc.
  • Nationality + Australia groups — "Germans in Australia", "Estonians in Australia", etc. Great for language and cultural support.
  • Farm work groups — for when you start your 88 days. "88 Days Farm Work Australia" is the biggest.
  • Accommodation groups — most major cities have specific backpacker housing groups.

Avoid groups that are 90% job postings — they're usually flooded with scams. Avoid groups that are 90% complaints — they kill your motivation. Look for active, helpful, balanced communities.

My experience

I joined about 4-5 of the right groups before I flew and it made a real difference. Found contacts for licence translation, accommodation tips, and got warned off a dodgy job before I made the mistake of taking it. Don't underestimate this step.

What you'll actually need for farm work:

  • Steel cap boots — required at most farms.
  • Long sleeve UV shirts — Australian sun is brutal.
  • Wide brim hat — not optional.
  • Heavy duty gloves — for picking work.
  • Sunscreen — strong stuff, use it every day.

But here's the thing nobody tells you: don't bother packing all this from home. Australia has Kmart. It's everywhere, it's cheap, and it has literally everything you need. Steel cap boots, work shirts, hats, gloves — all there, all affordable. Don't arrive with a massive heavy luggage full of stuff you could buy for less at the first Kmart you walk into.

Kmart is genuinely one of Australia's best kept secrets for backpackers. Cheap clothes, cheap kitchenware, cheap everything. It should be one of your first stops when you land.

My experience

I would not recommend packing heavy workwear from home. When I arrived I went to Kmart and found everything I needed at a fraction of what it would have cost to pack and carry it. Save the luggage space. Travel light. Buy it here.

Chapter 2

Your first week in Australia

The confusing bit. Do these in order and your first 7 days go from chaos to sorted.

Getting a local SIM card is the first thing you should do after landing. You need it for everything else — bank signup, job applications, navigating around, finding accommodation.

Three main networks in Australia:

  • Optus — best balance of price, coverage, and data. Strong in cities and most regional areas. $30-40/month for unlimited data.
  • Telstra — best coverage in remote and outback areas, but more expensive. Essential if you're doing farm work in really remote spots.
  • Vodafone — cheapest in major cities but coverage drops off fast outside them.

You can buy a prepaid SIM at the airport, in any Woolworths or Coles supermarket, or in dedicated phone shops. No contracts needed, no Australian ID required for prepaid.

My experience

We picked up a SIM at Perth Airport pretty much straight after landing. The convenience of having a working phone immediately is worth not shopping around — pick one and move on. You can always switch later if you need better coverage for farm work.

You need an Australian bank account before your first paycheck — no Australian employer will pay you into a foreign account. Get this done in your first week.

The two top picks for backpackers:

  • Up Bank — fully online, opens in 5 minutes, no monthly fees, brilliant app, instant card via Apple Pay or Google Pay.
  • Commonwealth Bank (CommBank) — biggest network of branches and ATMs. Better if you prefer in-person banking.

The address trick: Australian banks require an Australian address to open an account. Your hostel or Airbnb address works perfectly fine. Just write it down. They don't verify it at this stage.

My experience

This is the one nobody tells you about before you arrive. I was stressed about not having a "real" address yet. Turns out your Airbnb or hostel address works completely fine. I opened my account using our Joondalup Airbnb address and had it sorted within the first couple of days.

Your Tax File Number is the Australian equivalent of a social security number. You need it before you can be paid properly by any employer.

Without a TFN, employers must legally withhold 47% of your wages as tax. With a TFN, you're taxed normally — around 15% for most WHV workers. Massive difference.

How to get a TFN:

  • Go to ato.gov.au (Australian Taxation Office)
  • Click "Apply for a TFN"
  • Fill in the form — passport, address, basic details
  • TFN arrives by mail to your Australian address in 10-28 days

It is completely free. There is no rush option. There is no premium version.

My experience

Anyone charging you money to get a TFN is scamming you. There are agencies that charge $50-200 for "TFN application services". They literally just fill in the same free form on ato.gov.au and pocket your money. Do it yourself. It's a 10 minute government form.

Hostels and Airbnbs are fine for the first week but they're expensive and exhausting long-term. Within your first 2 weeks you want to move into a share house.

Where to look for share houses in Australia:

  • Facebook Marketplace — biggest platform for share houses by far. Filter by location and rent.
  • Flatmates.com.au — the most established Australian roommate site.
  • Facebook backpacker groups — city-specific backpacker housing groups are very active.
  • Gumtree — older platform, lower quality listings but still active.

Expect to pay 2-4 weeks rent as a bond upfront, plus the first 2 weeks rent. For a $250/week room, you're paying $1,000-1,500 to move in.

My experience

The accommodation situation in Perth was the most stressful part of our arrival. We ended up sleeping in our car for a period because we couldn't find anything affordable quickly enough. Book at least 2 weeks somewhere before you land — even if it's more expensive per night, the stress it saves is worth it. Then use those 2 weeks to properly search for a share house.

  • No tipping. Australians do not tip in restaurants, bars, taxis, or anywhere else. Staff are paid proper wages.
  • Tap and go everywhere. Even tiny shops accept contactless. Cash is rare. You can survive a year barely touching cash.
  • Prices include GST. What you see on the menu is what you pay. No mental math like in the US.
  • Minimum wage is AUD $24.10/hour. One of the highest in the world. This is why everything feels expensive — wages are too.
  • Weekend pay is higher. Penalty rates mean you can earn 1.5x or 2x normal pay on weekends in hospitality.
My experience

The no-tipping thing is genuinely strange when you're not used to it. I tipped my waitress at my first dinner and she looked confused. You really don't tip here. The tap-everywhere thing is amazing once you adjust — I used cash maybe a handful of times in an entire year.

Public transport in Sydney and Melbourne is genuinely good. In Perth and most other cities, it gets sparse fast — especially for getting to farm work or regional areas.

The backpacker car market is huge and well-established. Cars circulate from backpacker to backpacker and often hold value well.

Where to buy a car as a backpacker:

  • Facebook Marketplace — biggest backpacker car market.
  • Gumtree — older platform, still very active.
  • Hostel notice boards — backpackers leaving often sell to arriving backpackers.

Always get a PPSR check ($2 from ppsr.gov.au) before buying — confirms the car isn't stolen, written off, or under finance.

My experience

Getting a car changed everything for us in Perth. We found one through Facebook and suddenly we had freedom — places to go, a way to get to work, and honestly somewhere to sleep when accommodation fell through. In Perth especially, a car is not a luxury. It's essential. Budget for it early.

Most working holiday visa holders do not get Medicare (Australia's public healthcare). A few countries have reciprocal agreements — UK, Ireland, Netherlands, Sweden, Finland, Norway, Belgium, Slovenia, Malta, and Italy. Check if yours is one.

If you don't have Medicare:

  • Your travel insurance is your healthcare. Use it.
  • A standard GP appointment costs $80-150 out of pocket.
  • Ask for a bulk billing clinic — these are free or much cheaper.
  • Pharmacies (called "chemists") can handle a lot of minor issues without a doctor.
  • Emergency: call 000 (not 999 or 911).
My experience

Always ask "do you bulk bill?" when you call to book a GP. Could save you $100+ per visit. The emergency number being 000 is something to memorise on day one — it's different from what most Europeans and Americans expect.

Backpackers are a target because we're new, often desperate for work, and don't know what's normal. The main scams to watch out for:

  • "Placement fee" job scams — A "labour hire" company asks you to pay $100-500 upfront to be placed in a farm job. Real companies never charge workers.
  • Fake farm work — Job posts that don't actually exist. Verify with the farm directly before traveling.
  • TFN/visa "processing services" — Charging for things the government provides free.
  • Cash-in-hand only jobs — Can't prove your 88 days without payslips. Get an ABN and proper paperwork.
My experience

We nearly got caught by a "labour hire" company our second month. They wanted money upfront to "secure our placement" at a farm. Last minute we checked a Facebook group and several people said it was a known scam. Always check with the community before paying anyone anything.

Chapter 3

Finding work in Australia on a Working Holiday Visa

Realistic, honest guidance on getting your first Australian job — what works, what doesn't, and how to not get underpaid.

You can do almost any job on a working holiday visa, but you can only work for the same employer for 6 months. After that you must switch.

The most common jobs for backpackers in Australia:

  • Hospitality — cafes, restaurants, bars. Easiest to get with zero Australian experience. Good pay especially weekends.
  • Retail — clothing stores, supermarkets, electronics. Stable hours, decent pay.
  • Construction — labouring, traffic control, scaffolding. High pay but needs a "White Card" certificate ($60).
  • Farm work — required for your 88 days. Pay varies massively by farm.
  • Cleaning — hotels, offices, Airbnb turnovers. Easy to get, often cash in hand (risky).
My experience

We found farm work through connections made in Perth. The farm job gave us both income and our 88 days simultaneously — most efficient path if you can get it early. Hospitality in cities is the easiest to get quickly though.

The Australian CV (called a "resume") is dramatically simpler than what you'd write in most European countries. Trying to use a fancy European-style CV will actively hurt you.

The Australian resume format:

  • One page only. Two pages max if you have significant experience.
  • No photo. Considered unprofessional and potentially biased.
  • No personal details beyond name, phone, email, suburb.
  • Brief summary at the top — 2-3 lines about what you do.
  • Work experience — most recent first. Brief bullet points.
  • References at the bottom — "Available on request" or list 2 names.

Plain, clean formatting. Black text on white. No icons. No infographics. No progress bars for skills.

My experience

A mate of mine had a 3-page design-heavy European CV with a photo, skill bars, and color blocks. Got zero callbacks for 2 weeks. Rewrote it to a plain 1-page Australian format — immediate improvement. Same person, same experience, different presentation. Australia genuinely prefers plain and concise.

  • Seek.com.au — by far the biggest job site in Australia. Used by almost every employer.
  • Indeed.com.au — second biggest. Good for entry-level and casual work.
  • Gumtree — older, more casual. Watch for scams.
  • Backpacker Facebook groups — for hospitality, farm work, and quick gigs.
  • Walking in with a printed CV — still works in hospitality and retail. Tuesday-Wednesday mornings 10am-12pm is the sweet spot.

Application volume strategy: apply to 10+ jobs at once. Don't wait for replies before applying to more. Most won't respond. That's normal — keep going.

If you want to work in any venue that serves alcohol — bars, pubs, restaurants, nightclubs, hotels — you legally need an RSA (Responsible Service of Alcohol) certificate.

It's a one-day online or in-person course that teaches you Australian alcohol laws. Cost is $50-150 depending on state and provider.

  • Each state has its own RSA — NSW RSA doesn't work in Victoria, etc.
  • Online courses take 3-6 hours at your own pace.
  • Pick the state where you'll work most first.
My experience

The RSA is one of the best investments you can make early on. Bar and restaurant work pays well and is plentiful — especially on weekends when penalty rates kick in. Get it done in your first week or two.

Australian worker rights apply to you as a WHV holder exactly the same as to citizens. Some employers will try to underpay backpackers thinking we don't know our rights.

Key numbers as of 2026:

  • National minimum wage: AUD $24.10/hour
  • Casual loading: if you're casual (no sick leave or holiday pay), you get an extra 25% — minimum $30.13/hour.
  • Weekend penalty rates: Saturdays typically 1.25-1.5x, Sundays often 1.5-2x.
  • Public holidays: typically 2.5x normal pay.

The exact rates depend on your "Award" — the legal document covering your industry. The Fair Work website lets you look up your specific Award.

My experience

Know your rights before you accept any job. Check the Fair Work website for your industry's Award rates. If an employer offers you less than the legal minimum, you can report them to the Fair Work Ombudsman — even after you've left Australia.

This might be the most important step on this entire website. Read it carefully.

Superannuation is Australia's mandatory retirement savings system. By law, every employer must pay an extra 11% of your wages into a super fund on your behalf. This is on top of your wages, not deducted from them.

Here's what makes this incredible for backpackers: when you leave Australia permanently, you can claim every single cent of it back. It's called the Departing Australia Superannuation Payment (DASP) and you apply for it online after your visa expires.

It gets taxed at 65% when you claim it as a WHV holder — yes that's high. But it's still money you'd otherwise leave behind completely. Over a year of work, it adds up to hundreds or thousands of dollars.

What to do right now:

  • Choose a super fund before your first job — AustralianSuper, HostPlus, and REST are all fine choices
  • Give your employer your super fund details and member number when you start
  • Use the same fund for every job so your money is in one place
  • Keep your super fund login somewhere safe
  • When you leave Australia — apply for DASP at the ATO website

The number of backpackers who worked a full year in Australia and left without claiming their super is genuinely heartbreaking. Some have thousands of dollars sitting in Australian super funds right now they'll probably never see.

My experience

Superannuation was one of the biggest things I wish someone had explained to me properly from day one. Make sure every employer sets it up for you correctly. Keep all your payslips. And when you leave — claim every cent of it back.

The Australian tax year runs July to June. After June each year, you lodge a tax return between July and October. As a WHV holder, you often get money back.

WHV tax rates: 15% on the first $45,000 of income. Above that, normal Australian tax brackets apply.

To file your return:

  • Go to myGov and link it to the ATO
  • Use the myTax tool — free and pre-fills most info automatically
  • Lodge between July 1 and October 31
  • Refund usually arrives in your bank in 2-4 weeks
My experience

I was dreading my first Australian tax return. It took about 45 minutes on myTax. The ATO had already pre-filled my income from my employers. I confirmed the numbers, submitted, and got money back a couple of weeks later. Genuinely one of the easiest government processes I've dealt with.

An Australian Business Number (ABN) is needed if you're working as a contractor rather than an employee. Common situations where you'll need one:

  • Farm work where you're paid by the farmer directly (not through an agency)
  • Freelance work
  • Some labour hire arrangements
  • Driving for Uber, delivering for DoorDash, etc

Getting an ABN is free at abr.gov.au. Takes about 15 minutes and you get the number immediately or within a few days.

Important: Working under an ABN means you're responsible for your own tax and super. The employer doesn't withhold tax or pay super for you.

My experience

Some farms pay cash-in-hand but this creates problems for your 88-day proof. You can't show payslips because there are none. Get an ABN and ask for proper invoices — it takes 15 minutes and makes your 88-day documentation airtight.

Australia takes worker rights seriously and the Fair Work Ombudsman enforces them for ALL workers — including WHV holders, regardless of visa or nationality.

Things you can report:

  • Being paid less than minimum wage
  • Not getting penalty rates on weekends
  • Not being paid for hours actually worked
  • Not getting your final pay when you leave a job
  • Sexual harassment, bullying, or discrimination

You can report anonymously at fairwork.gov.au or call free on 13 13 94. Fair Work can take legal action on your behalf and recover wages — even after you've left Australia.

My experience

Don't let dodgy employers get away with it. They count on backpackers not knowing the rules or being too short-term to complain. Australia gives you real protection here — use it if you need it.

Chapter 4

The 88 days — farm work and visa extension

If you want a second year on your visa, you need 88 days of specified regional work. Here's how to do it without messing up.

Not all regional work counts toward your 88 days. Both the type of work and the location postcode must qualify.

Industries that count:

  • Agriculture (farming, fruit picking, livestock)
  • Fishing and pearling
  • Tree farming and felling (forestry)
  • Mining
  • Construction (in eligible regional areas only)
  • Plant and animal cultivation in specified regions

Eligible postcodes are listed on the Home Affairs website. Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, Gold Coast, and most other cities do not count. The work must be in a designated regional area.

Common ways this goes wrong:

  • Working at a farm that turns out to be just outside an eligible postcode
  • Doing hospitality at a regional hostel thinking it counts — it doesn't
  • Cash-in-hand work that can't be proven with payslips
My experience

Before taking any farm job, check the postcode on the Home Affairs website. The official list updates and you need to be sure. One wrong postcode and weeks of work don't count — don't be casual about this.

Finding farm work is one of the bigger logistical challenges of your WHV. The job market is real but full of scams and dodgy operators.

Where to look for genuine farm work in Australia:

  • Harvest Trail — Australian Government's official harvest jobs site. Verified farm work listings.
  • Backpacker Job Board — biggest platform for backpacker-specific jobs. Verified employers.
  • Facebook farm work groups — "88 Days Farm Work Australia" is the biggest. Verify any job before traveling for it.
  • Word of mouth in hostels — backpackers who just finished their 88 days can recommend good farms.

Red flags that mean it's a scam:

  • Any "placement fee" or upfront payment
  • Vague location or "we'll tell you when you arrive"
  • No verifiable farm name or ABN
  • Pressure to pay or commit quickly

Social media makes farm work look romantic and tan-filled. The reality is more physical and exhausting than most people expect.

  • Early starts. 4-5am wake-ups are common. Work starts at sunrise to beat the heat.
  • Physical work. Picking, lifting, bending, walking for 8-10 hours. You will be sore the first week.
  • Piece rates or hourly. Some farms pay per kg/box picked, others pay hourly.
  • On-site accommodation. Often shared rooms in farm-owned housing. Usually $100-200/week deducted from your pay.
  • Weather dependent. Rain days mean no work and no pay.

Realistic pay: $700-1,500 per week depending on the crop, farm, and your speed.

My experience

We found farm work and it solved two problems at once — income and the 88 days. The physical work was tough at first but your body adapts quickly. Push through the first 2 weeks and it gets much easier.

The 88 days must be proven with documentation when you apply for your second-year visa. If you can't prove it, those days don't count.

What to keep:

  • Every payslip — both digital and paper, backed up to cloud storage
  • Employer details — name, ABN, address, contact person
  • Date ranges for each job — first day, last day, total days worked
  • Form 1263 — official Australian Government "Employment Verification" form, get your employer to sign it
  • Bank statements showing wages received

The 88 days don't need to be consecutive. You can work at multiple farms across different months and add them up.

My experience

Keep a record of every single day you work — date, employer, what you did. Scan every payslip the day you get it. Ask every employer for Form 1263 before you leave. Some farms push back on signing it because it's a hassle for them. Don't leave without it.

You apply for your second-year visa through the same ImmiAccount portal as your first visa. The critical thing is timing.

  • Apply before your first visa expires — at least 4-6 weeks before
  • If your first visa expires while your second is being processed, you go onto a "bridging visa" and can stay but can't necessarily work
  • Processing usually takes 1-4 weeks but can be longer
  • Cost is $635 again

What you'll need:

  • Proof of 88 days work (payslips, employer declarations, bank statements)
  • Passport still valid for the full second year
  • Bank statement showing financial means

When you leave Australia permanently after your WHV ends, you can claim every cent of your superannuation back. It's called the Departing Australia Superannuation Payment (DASP).

How to claim:

  • Wait until your visa has expired or you've permanently departed Australia
  • Apply online through the ATO's DASP application system
  • Provide your super fund details (member number, fund name)
  • Payment is sent to your nominated bank account

Tax on DASP for WHV holders is 65%. High — but it's still 35% of money you'd otherwise leave behind forever. You must claim within 6 months of leaving or your super goes to the ATO as unclaimed money.

My experience

Most backpackers I've spoken to didn't know they could claim their super back. Don't leave Australia without doing this. Even a few hundred dollars is worth the 10 minutes it takes to apply.

Chapter 5

Living in Australia as a backpacker

The stuff that makes the experience good, not just survivable. Cities, cars, travel, mental health, emergencies.

Sydney

Most jobs, most expensive, best beaches and iconic sights. Big backpacker scene. Rent $250-400/week in a share house.

Melbourne

Cheaper than Sydney, world-class food and coffee, brilliant culture. Unpredictable weather. Rent $200-350/week.

Brisbane

Warm year round, more relaxed pace, growing fast. Less competition for housing and jobs. Rent $200-300/week.

Perth

Highest wages especially in mining and construction. Beautiful beaches. Very isolated — closest major city is Adelaide, a 28-hour drive away. Good if you want to save money fast.

My experience

I started in Perth and it was beautiful but isolated. Moving around Australia is part of the experience — don't lock yourself into one city. Each one has something different to offer and you have a whole year or more.

If you're doing farm work or want to explore the country, you'll need a car. The backpacker car market is huge — cars circulate from backpacker to backpacker and often hold value well.

Where to buy a car as a backpacker:

  • Facebook Marketplace — biggest backpacker car market
  • Gumtree — older platform, still active
  • Hostel notice boards — backpackers leaving often sell to arriving ones

Before buying any car:

  • PPSR check — $2 from ppsr.gov.au. Confirms car isn't stolen, written off, or under finance
  • Check registration (rego) has at least 3-6 months left
  • Get a roadworthy certificate (RWC) in most states
  • Always test drive it
My experience

Getting a car in Perth changed everything for us. Suddenly we had freedom — places to go, a way to get to work, and options we didn't have before. Budget for it early, especially in Perth. It's not a luxury there, it's essential.

Australia is enormous. Traveling around it is part of the WHV experience and there are several budget-friendly options.

  • Greyhound bus passes — multi-trip passes for the east coast. $300-800 depending on duration.
  • Jetstar — biggest budget airline. Flights as low as $40-80 between major cities if booked early.
  • Bonza — newer budget airline serving regional routes.
  • Relocate hire cars — rental companies pay you to drive their vehicles to another city. Can be almost free.
  • Carpool with other backpackers — Facebook groups have ride-share posts daily.
My experience

Book flights early. Jetstar domestic flights booked 6 weeks ahead can be $40-60. The same flight booked last week can be $250+. Plan ahead and domestic travel in Australia is genuinely affordable.

Nobody writes about this part because it's not exciting and it doesn't photograph well. But it's the most important chapter.

At some point in your first 2 months, you'll probably have a bad week. The excitement of arriving wears off. You're tired from working long shifts. You miss family. The realities of being far from home set in.

This is normal. Almost every backpacker goes through this.

What actually helps:

  • A routine. Same wake-up time, gym days, coffee shop. Routines anchor you.
  • A gym or sport. Physical activity is the most reliable mood medicine.
  • Regular contact with home. Weekly call with family. Just contact.
  • Honest conversations with other backpackers. Everyone is going through some version of this.
  • Limit social media. Comparing your day to friends' highlight reels is poison.

If things get serious: Lifeline Australia: 13 11 14. Free, confidential, 24/7.

My experience

The first weeks were hard — no stable accommodation, no income yet, unfamiliar everything. Getting the car helped because it gave us options. Getting farm work helped because it gave us structure and income. The bad periods are real but they pass. Don't make permanent decisions during temporary bad weeks.

Emergencies in Australia:

  • Police, ambulance, fire: 000 (not 911 or 999)
  • Non-emergency police: 131 444
  • Lifeline mental health crisis: 13 11 14
  • Medical advice line: 1800 022 222

If your passport is lost or stolen:

  • Report to local police first — get a police report number
  • Contact your country's embassy or consulate in Australia
  • Your visa is electronic — losing your passport doesn't cancel your visa

If your wallet is stolen:

  • Block all cards immediately through banking apps
  • File a police report — needed for travel insurance claims

If you have a visa issue:

  • Contact Home Affairs immediately on 131 881
  • Don't ignore visa expiry — overstaying has serious consequences
My experience

The emergency number being 000 is something to memorize the day you land. It's different from what most Europeans and Americans expect. Save it in your phone before you need it.

Once you're earning in Australia, you'll want to send some money home. Your Australian bank can do international transfers but the rates are typically poor and fees are high.

The best options for sending money from Australia:

  • Wise — best exchange rates, low flat fees, transfers arrive in 1-2 business days. This is what most backpackers use.
  • Revolut — similar to Wise, slightly different fee structure. Worth comparing.
  • Your bank's international transfer — usually worst rates and highest fees. Use as a last resort.

The difference in exchange rates between a bank transfer and Wise on a $1,000 AUD transfer can be $30-60 in your favour. Over a full year of sending money home, that adds up significantly.

My experience

I used Wise for all my transfers home and never had an issue. Set it up before you fly as I mentioned in Chapter 1 — then it's ready to use the moment you want to send money back.
